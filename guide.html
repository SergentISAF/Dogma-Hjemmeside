How to set up the website

A guide for Hans, written with no assumed knowledge.
One step at a time. You can't break anything.

What you need

  • An email address you actually check (e.g. hansmlauridsen@gmail.com)
  • About 100 kroner to buy a domain name
  • 30-60 minutes at the computer
  • A cup of coffee

You don't need any programming experience. We'll click through it together.

Important to know first: The website itself is already built and hosted free at GitHub. What we need to do now is connect your own domain name (e.g. dogmacables.com) to it, so people can find it. We do this through a free service called Cloudflare. Think of Cloudflare as the sign at the entrance to your driveway, telling people how to find you.
1

Buy your domain

A domain name is the address people type into their browser. For example dogmacables.com.

  1. Open a domain registrar in your browser. Good options: Cloudflare Registrar (cheapest), Namecheap, or DanDomain (Danish).
  2. Type the name you want, e.g. dogmacables.com, into the search field.
  3. If it's available, click Buy or Add to cart.
  4. Create an account with your email and pay. A .com domain costs about 80-100 kr per year.
Step 1 is done when: you've received a confirmation email saying you now own your domain.
2

Create a Cloudflare account

Cloudflare is a free service that helps direct people to the right website. It's free, and you won't pay anything.

  1. Open dash.cloudflare.com/sign-up.
  2. Enter your email and a password you'll remember. Write the password down somewhere you can find it again.
  3. Click Create Account.
  4. Cloudflare sends a confirmation email. Open your inbox, find the email, and click the link inside it.
Step 2 is done when: you're on a Cloudflare page that says "Add a site" or "Add a domain".
3

Add your domain to Cloudflare

  1. On the Cloudflare page, click + Add a domain (top right or on the homepage).
  2. Type your domain, e.g. dogmacables.com (without www in front), and click Continue.
  3. Choose the free plan: Free $0/month. Click Continue.
  4. Cloudflare now scans your domain. It takes 30-60 seconds. You don't need to do anything.
  5. When it's done, you'll land on a page with a list of DNS records. Skip ahead to step 5 to add the right ones, but first do step 4.
In a moment Cloudflare will give you two nameservers (they look like something.ns.cloudflare.com). Write them down or keep the page open. You'll need them in step 4.
4

Change nameservers at your registrar

This is the most important step. You're telling your domain "from now on, listen to Cloudflare, not the registrar". It's like moving your mail from one post office to another.

  1. Open a new tab and log in to your domain registrar (where you bought the domain).
  2. Find your domain in the list of your purchases and click on it.
  3. Look for a section called Nameservers or DNS or Set nameservers.
  4. Delete the nameservers listed there, and paste in the two Cloudflare gave you in step 3 (the ones ending in .ns.cloudflare.com).
  5. Click Save or Update.
  6. Go back to the Cloudflare tab and click Continue or Done, check nameservers.
Now wait. It can take anywhere from 15 minutes to 24 hours before Cloudflare can see the change. You'll get an email from Cloudflare when it's ready. You can carry on with step 5 in the meantime, but the website itself only works once Cloudflare confirms.
If you can't find a "Set nameservers" button: For new domains, the option can take 24-72 hours to appear at some registrars (DanDomain in particular). Call the registrar's support if you're in a hurry.
5

Add DNS records that point to GitHub

Now you tell Cloudflare where the website lives. The website is hosted free at GitHub.

  1. In Cloudflare, select your domain from the list.
  2. In the left menu, click DNS.
  3. Delete any existing A and CNAME records (it's normally safe to remove them if you've just bought the domain).
  4. Click + Add record and create four A records, one at a time. For each:
    • Type: A
    • Name: @ (this means "the domain itself")
    • IPv4 address: one of the four below
    • Proxy status: Proxied (orange cloud icon)
    • Click Save

    The four IP addresses from GitHub:

    • 185.199.108.153
    • 185.199.109.153
    • 185.199.110.153
    • 185.199.111.153
  5. Add one CNAME record for www:
    • Type: CNAME
    • Name: www
    • Target: sergentisaf.github.io
    • Proxy status: Proxied
    • Click Save
Step 5 is done when: you have 5 records in the list (4 A records and 1 CNAME record).
6

Tell GitHub about your domain

The last step is to tell GitHub: "people are reaching this website through dogmacables.com, so respond to that name".

  1. Open github.com/SergentISAF/Dogma-Hjemmeside/settings/pages.
  2. Log in if asked (Dan has the credentials).
  3. Find the field called Custom domain.
  4. Type your domain, e.g. dogmacables.com (without https:// in front).
  5. Click Save.
  6. GitHub checks your domain for 1-5 minutes. When there's a green checkmark next to the domain, it's approved.
  7. Tick Enforce HTTPS (this means the site is secure, with a padlock in the address bar).
Step 6 is done when: you can open a browser, type dogmacables.com, and see the website. If it says "Not secure" or "could not load", wait 10 minutes and try again. Cloudflare and GitHub need a moment to talk to each other.
7

Set up the contact form (Formspree)

Right now the contact form opens the visitor's mail app. It works, but it's clunky for the customer. With Formspree the messages arrive directly in your inbox without the customer having to do anything extra.

  1. Open formspree.io and click Sign up free.
  2. Use hansmlauridsen@gmail.com and create a password.
  3. Confirm your email (check your inbox, click the link).
  4. Inside Formspree, click + New Form.
  5. Give it a name, e.g. "Dogma Cables contact", and set hansmlauridsen@gmail.com as the recipient.
  6. Click Create Form.
  7. You'll get a Form ID that looks like this: xeoznkqr (8 random characters).
  8. Send the ID to Dan and he'll plug it into the website.
The free tier allows 50 emails per month. That's plenty to start. If you receive more than that, we can upgrade later.

What if it doesn't work?

  • Website shows "Not Found": Wait 30 minutes. GitHub and Cloudflare need to talk. If it still doesn't work, double-check that the four IP addresses in Cloudflare are correct.
  • "Not secure" warning: The SSL certificate isn't ready yet. Wait 1-2 hours.
  • Cloudflare says "nameservers not active": Wait. It can take up to 24 hours after you change nameservers.
  • Can't log in: Use the "forgot password" link.
  • Something else is wrong: Write or call Dan. Tell him what you did last and what you saw.

What do the words mean?

Domain
The address of your website, e.g. dogmacables.com. It's what people type into the browser.
DNS
Short for "Domain Name System". It's the internet's phone book. It says: "When somebody types dogmacables.com, send them to this computer".
Nameserver
The computer that decides where people are sent when they type your domain. We're moving that responsibility from your registrar to Cloudflare.
A record
A line in the DNS table that points your domain to a specific IP address (e.g. GitHub's).
CNAME record
A line that points one domain at another, instead of at an IP address. We use it to make www.dogmacables.com work as well.
SSL/HTTPS
The padlock that shows up in the browser's address bar. It means the connection is secure. GitHub and Cloudflare provide it free of charge.
Formspree
A service that receives contact form submissions and forwards them as emails to your inbox.
